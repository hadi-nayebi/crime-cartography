# Editorial north star — factual memory, made engaging

Crime Cartography is not a machine for turning datasets into motion. It is a
human-managed information system whose first job is to help people form a more
accurate, durable memory of recent history.

The language-model harness performs repeatable production work: research
support, data processing, comparison, drafting, visualization, assembly,
consistency checks, and revision routing. Humans supply what the machine is
missing: context, skepticism, taste, local knowledge, moral judgment, and the
decision that a five-minute video is genuinely worth a person's attention.

## The viewer promise

After approximately five minutes, a viewer should remember:

1. **time** — how the recorded pattern changed across recent decades;
2. **place** — how the pattern is distributed across the city;
3. **composition** — which categories explain the total when the source
   supports a defensible breakdown;
4. **context** — a few sourced historical anchors that help locate the chart in
   lived time; and
5. **limits** — what changed in the data, what is missing, and what the video
   cannot establish.

The video should be engaging because understanding accumulates, not because the
viewer is manipulated into waiting for an empty payoff.

## A healthy factual memory

People encounter a great deal of isolated information and very little durable
orientation. This project aims to provide a healthier factual information
diet: compact, sourced, visually legible accounts that help viewers place the
present inside a few decades of history.

Crime is the first domain. The operating format can later support public
health, housing, climate, migration, transportation, economics, education, or
other subjects where time, space, categories, and historical context improve
public understanding.

The method must preserve humility:

- a nearby historical event is a **memory anchor**, not automatically a cause;
- reported crime is not the same thing as crime committed, guilt, conviction,
  victimization, or public safety in full;
- a category or reporting-system change can move a chart without a comparable
  change in lived reality;
- missing data stays missing;
- spatial precision is never implied beyond what the source supports; and
- surprising claims receive more scrutiny, not more decorative certainty.

## The five-minute information architecture

Each city remake should earn its runtime through five linked questions:

1. **What is the long arc?**
   Show the citywide trend over the longest defensible comparable period.

2. **What changed in the ruler?**
   Mark missing years, taxonomy changes, agency transitions, boundary changes,
   and other measurement seams before inviting comparison.

3. **What makes up the total?**
   Show category composition or category-specific histograms when the source
   provides stable, meaningful categories. Do not manufacture comparability
   across incompatible taxonomies.

4. **Where is it happening?**
   Show recent spatial distribution at the most honest available resolution,
   with unmapped records and coverage limits disclosed.

5. **What should remain in memory?**
   Close with a small set of verified takeaways and sourced context anchors.
   The viewer should be able to explain the shape without remembering every
   number.

## Historical context anchors

A context anchor must include:

- a year or bounded date;
- a short viewer-facing label;
- a source;
- why the event is useful for orientation;
- whether it directly affects the measure or is context only; and
- an explicit prohibition on causal language unless evidence supports it.

Examples may include local policy changes, economic shocks, major technology
adoption, public-health events, agency reporting transitions, or nationally
shared events. An anchor is selected because it helps the viewer remember where
they are in time—not merely because it is famous.

## The human editorial layer

The human pass asks:

- What important context is absent?
- Which comparison is technically valid but mentally misleading?
- Which label, color, sound, or transition creates the wrong implication?
- Where does the video feel generic or interchangeable?
- Which fact deserves to become a memory anchor?
- What would a local resident, domain expert, data analyst, or storyteller
  notice that the production system missed?
- If the viewer remembers only three claims next week, are they the right ones?

The harness may propose answers. It may not certify its own taste, approve its
own consequential claims, or release its own work.

## Public machinery, controlled end product

The public project may include:

- source code and public harnesses;
- data provenance and transformations;
- schemas, rubrics, prompts, and validation machinery;
- video configurations, metadata, checksums, and release records;
- background research that can be lawfully shared; and
- the evolving project, contributor, and governance terms.

Finished videos, narration, scripts, music, thumbnails, channel artwork, and
other end-product editorial assets are not published in GitHub under the MIT
software license. Public reproducibility does not require surrendering the
rights to the end product.

## Success

A successful channel is not measured only by watch time or subscriber count.
It should show that:

- viewers retain correct, useful facts;
- the videos become more informative without becoming harder to follow;
- human contributions produce traceable improvements;
- repeated production becomes more reliable without becoming generic;
- corrections are visible and reusable; and
- the system can move to another factual domain without abandoning its
  standards.
