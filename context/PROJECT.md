# Project

Part of the [Crime Cartography shared context](INDEX.md).

## The project `[consolidated]`

Crime Cartography is a human-managed, agent-operated channel that turns sourced
crime data into visual stories about time, place, and composition.

Its first public experiment asks a practical question:

> Can a production harness do most repeatable work while people supply the
> context, skepticism, taste, and release judgment that the machine lacks?

Crime is the starting domain because public datasets make the production
process inspectable. The longer mission is to develop a reusable way to help
people form a healthier factual memory of recent history.

## The three project promises `[consolidated]`

1. **Useful before impressive.** A viewer should leave with a clearer mental
   model, not merely a moving chart.
2. **Human judgment before release.** Automation may propose, assemble, and
   check; a human remains accountable for consequential claims and publication.
3. **Public machinery, controlled end product.** The project publishes the
   reproducible system and decision record. Finished audiovisual works remain
   channel products outside the repository's MIT software license.

## How the project operates `[consolidated]`

### 1. Public face

The Academy project page explains the experiment, shows current state, and
routes visitors to the right source, Discussion, or email form.

### 2. Project body

This repository owns the shared context, data, provenance, transformation
code, video configurations, validation, public decision record, and release
gates.

### 3. Private operating body

The private harness runs locally first and may later move to one private VPS.
Email remains the default participant interface until a concrete need justifies
another service. Credentials, subscriber addresses, private reviews, and
unpublished media stay outside public Git.

## Roadmap `[consolidated structure; draft gates]`

The content lane and participation lane move independently. Publishing does not
wait for a participant threshold.

### Horizon 1 — prove the story

1. Open the public project room and email-request form.
2. Release the introduction and two human-approved dedicated remakes.
3. Publish what human feedback changed.

### Horizon 2 — prove the editorial loop

1. Hold a live Q&A and publish Project Update #2.
2. Test confirmation, privacy, task, scoring, appeal, and release controls.
3. At 500 project requests plus sufficient evidence, decide whether to open a
   capped email editorial cohort.

### Horizon 3 — earn expansion

1. Expand to more cities, comparisons, and crime-related formats.
2. Formalize governance and any value-distribution model only after qualified
   review and versioned consent.
3. Reuse the operating system for other factual domains when its safeguards
   have survived real operation.

The proposed Q&A trigger—two approved pilots plus 30 substantive public
contributions across three expertise lanes—is still a draft. The proposed
100-person first cohort is also a draft. They are questions for the public
decision rooms, not settled milestones.

## Project boundaries `[consolidated]`

- YouTube subscriptions, likes, comments, views, and shares are not paid
  project activity and do not determine project rewards.
- Reported crime is not crime committed, guilt, conviction, victimization, or
  public safety in full.
- Public comments are feedback, not commands.
- No agent can approve its own consequential claim, upload, compensation, or
  governance change.
- No economic language becomes operative without an effective version,
  participant consent, and any required legal, tax, privacy, accounting, and
  worker-classification review.
